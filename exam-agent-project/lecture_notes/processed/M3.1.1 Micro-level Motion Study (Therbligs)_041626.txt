What is Motion Study?
• Motion study aims to find the preferred method of doing 
work 
• Focuses on eliminating unnecessary motions 
• Improves efficiency and reduces fatigue

--- Page 3 ---
Two Levels of Motion Study
• Motion study can be approached at two levels: 
– Micro-level analysis 
– Process-level analysis 
• Today’s focus: Micro-level analysis

--- Page 4 ---
What is Micro-Level Analysis?
• Focuses on human body movements 
• Breaks work into smallest motion units 
• Useful for repetitive manual tasks 
• Enables precise improvement

--- Page 5 ---
Micro Motion Study
• Technique for analyzing detailed human motions 
• Often uses video or observation 
• Based on Therbligs 
• Helps identify inefficiencies

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• RE (Reach)
– Also called Transport Empty (TE)
– Motion performed with an empty hand 
– From the start of movement to arrival/stop 
– Example: Reaching for a pen
Fitts’ law

--- Page 10 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• G (Grasp)
– Motion of closing the hand to take control of an object 
– From initial contact to full control 
– Influenced by: 
• Type of object 
• Shape and weight 
– Example: Grasping a pen 
– Improvement ideas:
• Contact grasp (without lifting) 
• Use of vacuum, magnets, conveyors 
• Modify surface material (e.g., sponge) 
• Utilize pre-positioning (PP)

--- Page 11 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• M (Move)
– Moving an object while holding it 
– The time required is affected by: 
• Distance 
• Weight 
• Type of movement 
– Example: Moving a pen to the paper 
– Improvement ideas:
• Mechanization 
• Elimination of unnecessary movement 
• Reduction of distance and weight 
• Elimination of abrupt changes in direction 
• Allowing sufficient tolerance at the stopping point

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• RL (Release)
– The motion of releasing an object that has been grasped 
– Occurs in a very short moment 
– Improvement ideas:
• Releasing during movement 
• Mechanization

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• S (Search)
– An eye or hand motion used to locate an object 
– From the beginning of hesitation to the stopping point 
– Improvement ideas:
• Use of color coding 
• Labels 
• Better lighting 

--- Page 14 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• SL (Select)
– The motion of choosing one item from several alternatives 
– Sometimes S (Search) and SL (Select) occur simultaneously 
– Examples: 
• Selecting one pencil from a pencil case 
• Selecting one drill bit from a set of bits 
– Improvement ideas:
• Separate items by type 
• Increase compatibility or standardization

--- Page 15 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• P (Position)
– The motion of placing or orienting an object into the correct position 
– Includes aligning, turning, or fitting into place 
– Examples: 
• Positioning a key or a nail 
• Inserting a subway ticket (in older systems) 
– Improvement ideas:
• Eliminate unnecessary positioning 
• Use chamfers (a slanted edge that replaces a sharp corner) or 
guides to make positioning easier


--- Page 16 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• PP (Pre-Position)
– Placing an object in a predetermined location to facilitate the next 
positioning (P) motion 
– Makes subsequent actions faster and easier 
– Example: 
• Using a pen holder 
– Improvement ideas:
• Use devices such as a spring tool holder (spring balancer)
• Arrange tools in fixed, accessible locations

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• A (Assemble)
– The motion of joining two or more parts together 
– From the moment the parts come into contact
to the completion of assembly 
– Example: 
• Assembling a pen cap with a pen
• DA (Disassemble)
– The reverse motion of assembly 
– Involves separating previously joined parts

--- Page 18 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• AD (Avoidable Delay)
– Delay or interruption that can be controlled by the worker 
– Often caused by personal or behavioral factors 
– Examples: 
• Coughing 
• Personal habits

--- Page 19 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• UD (Unavoidable Delay)
– Delay or interruption beyond the worker’s control 
– Caused by external factors 
– Examples: 
• Delay in material supply 
• Machine waiting time 
– Improvement ideas:
• Improve the process or system 
• Address issues at the workflow level rather than the individual 
level

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• U (Use)
– The motion of using a tool 
– Involves performing work with equipment 
– Improvement ideas:
• Use jigs and fixtures 
• Use powered tools 
• Improve tool design

--- Page 21 ---
Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• H (Hold)
– Maintaining an object in position after grasping 
– Occurs when one hand holds while the other performs a task 
– Example: 
• Holding a bolt while tightening a nut 
– Improvement ideas:
• Use tools or fixtures 
• Use both hands effectively 
• Utilize friction or support mechanisms

Therbligs (Basic Motion Elements)
• PL (Plan)
– Mental activity involved in deciding the next action
• I (Inspect)
– Checking or verifying the quality or condition
• R (Rest)
– Recovery from fatigue 
– Necessary for maintaining performance and safety

*Goal: eliminate or reduce waste


--- Page 24 ---
Principles of Motion Economy
• Minimize the number and distance of motions
• Use both hands simultaneously and symmetrically 
• Perform finger motions from outside toward the body for 
better accuracy and strength 
• Use smooth and continuous motions
• Design motions that can be easily learned and habitual
• Minimize control motions (e.g., carrying a cup of coffee 
carefully) 
• Reduce eye fatigue
• Use different parts of the body alternately to avoid fatigue

Example – Task Improvement Using Therbligs
• Task: Charging a smartphone 
• Objective: Analyze and improve the task using Therbligs 
• Focus: 
– Identify non-productive motions 
– Redesign the task to improve efficiency

--- Page 27 ---
Task Scenario (Before Improvement)
• A user plugs in a smartphone to charge 
• Charging cable is placed on the desk 
• Multiple cables may be present 
• Connector requires precise alignment

--- Page 28 ---
Therblig Analysis (Before)
• S (Search): Look for charging cable 
• SE (Select): Choose correct cable 
• RE (Reach): Reach for cable 
• G (Grasp): Pick up cable 
• M (Move): Move cable toward phone 
• P (Position): Align connector 
• I (Inspect): Check orientation 
• Repeat P + I if misaligned 
• RL (Release): Release cable 

--- Page 29 ---
Problems Identified
• Presence of non-productive motions: 
– Search (S), Select (SE) 
– Position (P), Inspect (I) 
• Repeated alignment attempts 
• High precision required 
• Increased cognitive load

--- Page 30 ---
Improvement Idea
• Introduce magnetic charging system (e.g., MagSafe) 
• Use fixed cable location 
• Enable automatic alignment


--- Page 31 ---
Therblig Analysis (After Improvement)
• RE (Reach) 
• G (Grasp) 
• M (Move) 
• RL (Release) 
*Position and Inspect are eliminated

--- Page 33 ---
Key Insight
• Good design eliminates non-productive motions 
• Focus on: 
– Removing unnecessary steps 
– Simplifying interaction 
• Design can replace human effort

--- Page 34 ---
Design Principles Applied
• Eliminate Search → fixed location 
• Eliminate Select → standardization 
• Reduce Position → magnetic guidance 
• Reduce Inspect → automatic feedback
